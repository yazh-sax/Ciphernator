• Ciphers
















The Caesar Cipher is one of the earliest and most widely used ciphers in the world. The cipher is a substitution cipher that shifts each letter of a message by a fixed shift, so that the letter “L” with a shift of two would encrypt to “N”. This shifting process repeats for each letter in the message to produce the encrypted message. The cipher gets its name from the Roman general Julius Caesar, who used it to encrypt important messages.

This tool allows you to both encrypt and decrypt messages with a set shift, by shifting each letter forward by the inputted shift to encrypt, and backward to decrypt. The codebreaking algorithm uses a word bank of nearly 10,000 words, and compares potential shifts to the wordbank. Then, it determines the potential shift that decrypts the message in a manner which has the most matches with the word bank, breaking the encryption.
